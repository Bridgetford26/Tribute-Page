Some of her life and accomplishments

  • She was born October 11, 1884.
  • She was married to Franklin D. Roosevelt on March 17, 1905.
  • She was the longest serving First Lady of the United States.
  • She was a controversial First Lady for her time because of her outspokenness, especially on civil rights for African-Americans.
  • She was the first presidential spouse to hold regular press conferences and in 1940 became the first to speak at a national party convention.
  • She gave most of the money she earned from her lectures and writing to charity.
  • She spent the rest of the years after her husband died devoted to human rights around the world.
  • She was heavily influenced by all girls boarding school that she went to, French headmistress Marie Souvestre
  • During World War I, she visited wounded soldiers and worked for the Marine Corps Relief Society, and in a Red Cross canteen.
  • She held press conferences where she only invited women reporters, thus ensuring that newspapers employed women correspondents
  • Beginning in 1936 she wrote a daily syndicated newspaper column "My Day"
  • In 1961, President John F. Kennedy appointed her chair of his Commission on the Status of Women, which she continued with until shortly before her death.
  • She passed away November 7, 1962
  • She is buried at Hyde Park, her husband's family home on the Hudson River and the site of the Franklin D. Roosevelt Library.
